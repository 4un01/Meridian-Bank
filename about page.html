About Us

Some history about Meridian Bank

Meridian Bank was founded in 1882 in the heart of Leicester by two brothers, Edmund and Charles Hargreaves, who recognised a growing need for reliable and accessible financial services in the rapidly industrialising East Midlands region. At a time when the textile and hosiery industries were booming across Leicestershire, local businesses and workers alike struggled to find a trustworthy institution to safeguard their earnings and provide the credit necessary to grow. Edmund, a former accountant with sharp commercial instincts, and Charles, a well-respected figure in the local business community, pooled their savings and secured a modest premises on Granby Street, Leicester, opening the doors of what was then simply known as Hargreaves & Sons Banking House.
The early years were not without their difficulties. Britain in the 1880s was a nation of considerable economic uncertainty, with periodic downturns threatening the livelihoods of working families across the country. Despite this, the Hargreaves brothers built a reputation for fairness, transparency and genuine care for their customers. Unlike many of the larger London-based institutions of the era, Hargreaves & Sons made a deliberate choice to remain rooted in the local community, offering personal loans to tradespeople, savings accounts to factory workers, and commercial credit to the growing number of small business owners establishing themselves across the region. Word spread quickly, and within five years of opening, the bank had expanded to a second branch in Nottingham.
The turn of the twentieth century brought significant change to the British banking landscape. Consolidation was rife, with dozens of smaller regional banks being absorbed by larger national institutions. The Hargreaves family faced considerable pressure to sell during this period, receiving several approaches from competitors who recognised the strength of their local customer base. Edmund Hargreaves, now in his sixties and the dominant force behind the business following Charles's retirement due to ill health, famously refused every offer, reportedly telling one would-be acquirer that "a bank is only as strong as the community it serves, and we serve Leicester." This commitment to independence would define Meridian Bank's character for generations to come.
Following Edmund's death in 1911, leadership of the bank passed to his son, George Hargreaves, who had trained as a solicitor before joining the family business in 1905. George proved to be an astute and forward-thinking leader, guiding the bank through the turbulent years of the First World War with remarkable steadiness. During this period, Meridian — as it had come to be known informally among its customers — played a vital role in supporting the families of soldiers deployed overseas, offering deferred payment schemes and emergency credit lines that helped thousands of East Midlands households keep their heads above water. The bank also provided financing to several local manufacturers who had pivoted to wartime production, cementing its status as an indispensable pillar of the regional economy.
The interwar period saw Meridian Bank undergo its first major rebrand. In 1923, George Hargreaves formally renamed the institution Meridian Bank, choosing the name to reflect the bank's central position within the East Midlands and its ambition to be the defining financial institution of the region. A new headquarters was commissioned on Belvoir Street in Leicester, a grand neoclassical building that still stands today and serves as the bank's flagship branch. The building was widely admired upon its completion in 1925 and was celebrated in the local press as a symbol of confidence and permanence at a time when many were still recovering from the economic aftershocks of the war. The Great Depression of the 1930s tested Meridian Bank more severely than any previous challenge. As unemployment soared across Britain and businesses collapsed at an alarming rate, the bank found itself navigating an extraordinarily difficult environment. George Hargreaves, by then in his late fifties, took the controversial decision to absorb losses rather than call in loans from struggling customers, believing that protecting the local economy in the short term would ultimately benefit the bank over the long term. This approach drew criticism from some shareholders but earned enormous loyalty from the communities Meridian served, and the bank emerged from the depression with its reputation not just intact but significantly enhanced.
George Hargreaves retired in 1947 and was succeeded by his daughter, Margaret Hargreaves, making Meridian Bank one of the very few British financial institutions of the era to be led by a woman. Margaret's appointment was groundbreaking and not without controversy — several senior figures within the bank resigned in protest, reflecting the deeply entrenched gender biases of the time. However, Margaret proved to be an exceptional leader, arguably the most transformative in the bank's history. She oversaw Meridian's expansion throughout the postwar decades, opening sixteen new branches across the East Midlands between 1948 and 1965 and introducing a range of new products designed to serve the needs of a rapidly changing society. Under her leadership, Meridian became one of the first regional banks in Britain to offer mortgage products to working-class families, helping thousands of people across Leicestershire, Nottinghamshire and Derbyshire to buy their first homes during the postwar housing boom.
The 1960s and 1970s brought further transformation. Margaret Hargreaves, recognising the growing importance of technology in banking, invested heavily in early computing systems, making Meridian one of the first regional banks in Britain to automate its back-office processes. This investment in technology allowed the bank to process transactions faster, reduce errors, and ultimately offer better rates to its customers. It also positioned Meridian well for the deregulation of British financial markets that would follow in the 1980s. Margaret retired in 1978, having served as chief executive for over thirty years, and was awarded an OBE for her services to the financial sector and the East Midlands community.
The 1980s were a period of rapid change across British banking. The deregulation ushered in by the Thatcher government opened up new opportunities but also intensified competition, as building societies converted to banks and foreign institutions entered the British market for the first time. Under the leadership of Robert Ashworth, who became chief executive in 1979, Meridian Bank responded by doubling down on its regional identity and customer service proposition. Rather than competing directly with the large national banks on price, Ashworth positioned Meridian as the premium choice for East Midlands customers who valued a personal relationship with their bank. This strategy proved successful, and the bank grew its customer base by over forty percent during the 1980s despite the intensely competitive environment. The 1990s brought the digital revolution to banking, and Meridian was once again at the forefront of technological adoption among regional institutions. The bank launched its first telephone banking service in 1993 and its first internet banking platform in 1998, remarkably early for an institution of its size. These investments required significant capital and caused short-term pressure on the bank's finances, but they established Meridian as a technologically progressive institution that could compete with the major national banks on convenience while retaining the personal touch that had always set it apart. The global financial crisis of 2008 was perhaps the greatest test Meridian Bank had faced since the Great Depression. While many larger institutions required government bailouts and several collapsed entirely, Meridian's conservative approach to lending and its strong regional deposit base meant it weathered the storm without requiring external support. Chief executive Sarah Brennan, who had taken the role in 2004, was widely praised for her cautious stewardship during this period. The bank's decision to avoid the complex financial instruments that proved so toxic for many larger institutions was vindicated spectacularly, and in the years following the crisis Meridian attracted tens of thousands of new customers disillusioned with the big banks and looking for a more trustworthy alternative. Today, Meridian Bank serves over half a million customers across the East Midlands and beyond, managing assets in excess of £2.4 billion. It employs over three thousand people across its network of branches, offices and digital operations, making it one of the largest private employers in the region. The bank remains independently owned, still under the stewardship of the Hargreaves family through a trust established by Margaret Hargreaves before her death in 1994. It has received numerous awards in recent years for its customer service, its digital innovation and its commitment to the communities it serves, including being voted the leading bank in Leicestershire for five consecutive years.
Through more than 140 years of British history — through wars, recessions, technological revolutions and profound social change — Meridian Bank has remained true to the simple principle that Edmund and Charles Hargreaves set out with in 1882: that a bank exists to serve its community, not the other way around. That principle, as relevant today as it was in Victorian Leicester, is the foundation upon which everything Meridian Bank has achieved has been built, and it is the value that will guide the institution into whatever the future may bring.

What we offer

At Meridian Bank, our mission has always been simple: to provide the people and businesses of the East Midlands with the financial tools, guidance and support they need to build better lives. We are not simply a place to store money — we are a partner in the financial journey of every customer we serve, from the moment they open their first account to the day they retire and beyond.
At the heart of everything we do is personal banking. We offer a full range of current and savings accounts designed to suit every stage of life, whether you are a student managing your finances for the first time, a young professional building your savings, or a family planning for the future. Our savings accounts offer competitive interest rates, including our highly regarded ISA products, which allow customers to grow their money in a tax-efficient way. We believe that saving should be accessible to everyone, not just the wealthy, and we work hard to ensure our products reflect that belief.
For those looking to get onto the property ladder or remortgage their existing home, Meridian Bank offers a comprehensive range of mortgage products tailored to individual circumstances. Our dedicated mortgage advisors work one-on-one with customers to find the right solution for their situation, whether they are first-time buyers, home movers or looking to release equity from an existing property. We understand that buying a home is one of the most significant financial decisions a person will ever make, and we take that responsibility seriously.
Beyond personal banking, Meridian Bank is proud to be one of the leading business banking providers in the East Midlands. We support businesses of every size, from sole traders just starting out to established enterprises with complex financial needs. Our business banking services include commercial current accounts, business loans, asset financing, invoice financing and dedicated relationship managers who understand the unique challenges of running a business in today's economy. We have helped thousands of local businesses grow, adapt and thrive, and supporting the regional economy remains one of our most deeply held commitments.
We also offer a full range of lending products for personal customers, including personal loans and credit cards with competitive rates and flexible repayment terms. Whether a customer needs to finance a new car, consolidate existing debts or cover an unexpected expense, Meridian Bank provides straightforward, transparent lending with no hidden fees and no small print surprises. Financial planning and investment services form another key pillar of what we offer. Our qualified financial advisors help customers plan for long-term goals including retirement, inheritance planning and wealth management. We believe that professional financial advice should not be the exclusive preserve of the very wealthy, and we work hard to make expert guidance accessible to all of our customers regardless of the size of their portfolio.
Underpinning all of this is our unwavering commitment to security and trust. Every account held with Meridian Bank is protected by the Financial Services Compensation Scheme up to £85,000, and our dedicated fraud prevention team works around the clock to keep our customers' money safe. In a world of increasing financial complexity and digital risk, we take our responsibility to protect our customers with the utmost seriousness. Meridian Bank exists to make finance simple, fair and human. Whatever your financial goals, we are here to help you reach them.
